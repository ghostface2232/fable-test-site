N.003BREAKTHROUGHSN.003

Thirteen moments
that forged the world

  1. c.1200 BC First Ironworks Anatolian smiths smelt bloomery iron. The Bronze Age quietly ends.
  2. c.300 BC Wootz & Damascus South Indian crucibles yield patterned high-carbon steel, traded across empires.
  3. 1740 Crucible Steel Benjamin Huntsman remelts blister steel in Sheffield — uniform, clean, castable.
  4. 1784 Puddling Henry Cort's reverberatory furnace industrializes wrought iron at scale.
  5. 1856 The Bessemer Converter Air blown through molten iron makes steel in minutes, not weeks. Cost falls by ~80%.
  6. 1865 Open Hearth Siemens–Martin regenerative furnaces bring tonnage, patience and control.
  7. 1900 Electric Arc Héroult's EAF melts scrap with pure current. Recycling becomes an industry.
  8. 1913 Stainless Harry Brearley adds 12% chromium. Steel stops rusting; kitchens and clinics change forever.
  9. 1934 Electrical Steel Goss orients the grain. Transformers hum; the grid grows up.
  10. 1952 Basic Oxygen Linz & Donawitz blow pure O₂ — 300 tonnes of steel in forty minutes.
  11. 1959 Continuous Casting The endless strand replaces the ingot. Yield leaps; mills never sleep again.
  12. 2004 High-Entropy Alloys Cantor & Yeh mix five metals in equal parts. A new alloy universe opens.
  13. 2021 Fossil-Free Steel HYBRIT ships the first hydrogen-reduced steel to Volvo. The green ferrous era begins.

N.007QUESTIONSN.007

FAQ

Three thousand years of iron leave questions. We collected the ones we hear most — and answered them plainly.

QUESTION 01/03

Why did steel need three thousand years?

Temperature. Bloomery fires never fully melted iron, so smiths hammered sponge metal for millennia. Only when furnaces crossed ~1,500 °C — and Bessemer's air blast turned carbon itself into fuel — did liquid steel become an industrial material rather than a craft secret.

QUESTION 02/03

What actually makes steel “green”?

Replacing coke as the reducing agent. Hydrogen made with renewable power pulls oxygen out of ore and leaves only water vapour; electric-arc furnaces then melt that iron — or scrap — with clean electricity. Same steel, a fraction of the carbon.

QUESTION 03/03

Is anything left to invent in a 3,000-year-old material?

Almost everything. High-entropy alloys explore trillions of unmade recipes; amorphous metals freeze atoms mid-chaos; machine-learning models now design microstructures atom-first. Steel is old the way language is old — still being written.
